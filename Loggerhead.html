Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Caretta caretta

An interactive guide for Alabama's high school AI researchers.

Meet the Loggerhead

The Loggerhead is the most abundant sea turtle species in U.S. waters. They are named for their massive heads and powerful jaws, which are perfectly adapted for crushing the shells of their prey, like conchs and crabs.

  • Average Adult Size: ~3 feet carapace length
  • Average Adult Weight: 170 to 350 pounds
  • Lifespan: 70+ years
  • Conservation Status: Threatened / Endangered

Habitat & Alabama Presence

Global Habitat

Loggerheads are true ocean travelers, found in temperate and tropical waters of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. Hatchlings drift in the open ocean, often finding shelter in Sargassum seaweed. As they mature, they migrate to nearshore coastal areas like bays and estuaries to forage for food.

In Alabama's Waters

The Loggerhead is the most common sea turtle to nest on Alabama's shores, primarily on the beaches of Baldwin and Mobile counties. The Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge is a key nesting site. The nesting season runs from May through October.

The Nesting Ritual

Females often return to the beach where they were born to lay eggs. At night, they dig a flask-shaped nest with their rear flippers and lay 100-120 eggs.

Temperature-Dependent Sex: A fascinating fact is that the sand's temperature determines the sex of the hatchlings. Warmer nests produce more females, while cooler nests produce more males.

Movement & Locomotion

Graceful in Water

In water, loggerheads are powerful swimmers. They use their long front flippers for propulsion, moving them in a figure-eight motion like a bird's wings. Their hind flippers act as rudders for steering.

Laborious on Land

On land, their movement is much more difficult. They use a diagonal gait, moving opposite flippers in unison (e.g., front-right and back-left) to pull their heavy bodies across the sand to nest.

Footprints for Your AI Model

Loggerhead tracks are distinctive, showing an alternating pattern from their diagonal gait. The tracks are typically 2.5 to 3 feet wide, with comma-shaped marks from the front flippers.
