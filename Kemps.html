Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

(Lepidochelys kempii)

Endangered Species Profile for Alabama High School AI Class

Species Information

The Kemp's Ridley is the smallest and most critically endangered sea turtle in the world. Its diet consists mainly of crabs, but it also eats fish, jellyfish, and mollusks.

  • Size: Adults average 2 feet in length.
  • Weight: 70-100 pounds.
  • Color: Olive-gray shell with a creamy white bottom shell.
  • Lifespan: Estimated to be at least 30 years, possibly up to 50.

Habitat: Where They Live

Kemp's Ridley turtles prefer shallow, coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico and the Atlantic Ocean.

They are often found in bays, lagoons, and estuaries where their primary food source, blue crabs, are abundant. Younger turtles often use mats of sargassum (a type of seaweed) for shelter and food.

Found in Alabama

In Alabama, Kemp's Ridley sea turtles are occasional visitors to coastal waters, including Mobile Bay.

While most nest in Mexico, a few nests have been documented on Alabama's beaches, particularly in the Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge. Their presence here is often noted through stranding data.

Nesting Habits & "Arribada"

Kemp's Ridleys have a unique synchronized nesting behavior called an "arribada" (Spanish for "arrival"), where large groups nest at the same time.

  • Season: April to July
  • Time: Primarily during the day
  • Clutch Size: ~100 eggs per nest
  • Incubation: 45-60 days

A Life of Travel

Kemp's Ridley turtles are highly migratory. After hatching, they travel from nesting beaches to foraging grounds throughout the Gulf of Mexico and up the Atlantic coast, returning to the area where they were born to reproduce.

Tracking and Footprints

Tracking sea turtles is crucial for conservation. The tracks, or "crawl," they leave in the sand are a key indicator for researchers.

The crawl of a Kemp's Ridley is an alternating pattern, as they move their front flippers one at a time, which helps in identifying the species.
